The UAE is quietly becoming one of the most exciting toy markets on the planet. In 2025, the country's toy market reached an estimated $449 million — and that number is accelerating. Across the broader Middle East, the toy market sits at $4.6 billion and is projected to nearly double to $9.2 billion by 2034, according to multiple industry reports.

For anyone in the designer toy and collectibles space, this isn't just a data point. It's a signal. The region's unique blend of young demographics, high spending power, and rapidly expanding retail infrastructure is creating the perfect conditions for a collectibles boom. And at the center of it all? The kidult.

## The Numbers Tell the Story

Let's start with the macro picture. The global toy industry has been resilient through economic turbulence, but the Middle East stands out for its growth trajectory. At $4.6 billion in 2025, the regional market is growing at a compound annual rate that outpaces most mature markets in North America and Europe.

Within that, the UAE punches well above its weight. With a population of roughly 10 million, the $449 million figure translates to one of the highest per-capita toy spending rates in the world. That's not accidental. It reflects a culture that values gifting, collecting, and — increasingly — self-expression through design objects.

Saudi Arabia adds further momentum. Its own toy market is growing rapidly as Vision 2030 diversification efforts fuel consumer spending and retail expansion. Together, the UAE and Saudi Arabia form the engine of Middle East toy growth.

## Why the Kidult Market Matters Most

Here's the number that should make every toy brand pay attention: adults now account for the fastest-growing segment of the global toy market. Dubbed the "kidult" trend, this phenomenon refers to adults buying toys and collectibles for themselves — anime figures, blind box series, designer art toys, and limited-edition collaborations.

In the GCC region, this trend is supercharged. Roughly 60–80% of the population is under 30 years old. This isn't a region with a thin youth demographic on top of an aging base. It *is* the youth demographic. And those young consumers aren't just tech-savvy — they're culturally fluent, globally connected, and willing to spend on products that reflect their identity.

Blind box culture, which originated in Japan and China, has exploded across the Gulf. Walk through any Dubai mall and you'll find dedicated Pop Mart stores, figure shops, and pop-culture retailers doing brisk business. The social element — unboxing videos, trading communities, limited-run hunts — maps perfectly onto a region that prizes exclusivity and community.

This is where designer toys sit at the intersection of art, fashion, and collectibility. They're not children's playthings. They're cultural objects. And the Gulf's young, affluent consumers recognize that instinctively.

## Retail Infrastructure Is Booming

Demand means nothing without distribution — and the UAE is building at scale. In 2026 alone, seven new malls are set to open across the Emirates, adding millions of square feet of retail space to an already dense landscape.

The crown jewel, Dubai Mall, is undergoing a massive $1.5 billion expansion that will make it even larger as the world's most-visited shopping destination. For designer toy brands, this matters enormously. Physical retail isn't dead in the Gulf — it's the primary discovery channel. Consumers browse, touch, and buy in person, often making impulse purchases driven by visual merchandising and limited-edition drops.

Pop-up concepts and experiential retail also thrive in this market. The UAE's tourism sector — 17+ million visitors annually — means foot traffic extends far beyond the resident population. A well-placed pop-up or flagship display captures tourists, residents, and the region's growing base of digital nomads and remote workers.

## The Cultural Alignment Is Real

Beyond demographics and retail, there's a deeper cultural fit for designer toys in the Gulf. The region has a longstanding appreciation for luxury objects, craftsmanship, and limited-edition collectibles. From watches to sneakers to rare fragrances, GCC consumers understand the language of scarcity and design.

Designer toys speak that same language. A limited-run art figure with hand-painted details and numbered packaging? That's not a toy — that's a collectible. And Gulf consumers are already fluent in the culture of collecting.

There's also a growing local art scene feeding into the space. UAE-based artists, illustrators, and designers are creating original characters and collaborating with toy brands. This homegrown creativity gives the collectibles market authenticity and local relevance — it's not just imported culture, it's something that resonates with regional identity.
